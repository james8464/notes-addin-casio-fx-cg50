PROGRAM_LIMITATIONS.txt
CASIO symbolic programs — known unsupported forms & weak spots (2026-04-30)

This file catalogs expression/equation TYPES that the current programs:
  - cannot solve/simplify in their intended “exam working” style, OR
  - can solve mathematically but are too complex to present reliably within the
    calculator-style working/output constraints (timeouts / huge lines), OR
  - are intentionally out-of-scope (special functions, full CAS features, etc.).

Programs covered
----------------
- src/Math/algebraProgram.py
- src/Math/intProgram.py
- src/Math/deriveProgram.py
- src/Math/trigProgram.py
- src/Math/SUVATprogram.py
- src/ComputerScience/booleanProgram.py

The calculator wrappers in src/calc_files/*.py are just launchers.


GLOBAL / CROSS-PROGRAM LIMITATIONS
---------------------------------
1) General scope
   - Not a full CAS; aims at exam-style working for common patterns.
   - Piecewise / conditional expressions are not supported.
   - Complex-number solution sets are usually not produced (real-first behaviour).
   - Inequalities are mostly out of scope (except interval filtering in some solve modes).
   - General systems of equations are limited; multi-variable problems are often out of scope.

2) Exam-working formatting limits
   - Some transformations are intentionally compact; may omit mark-scheme “every line”.
   - Simplification preferences are heuristic; correct answers can appear in a different form.
   - Domain exclusions introduced by squaring / clearing denominators / substitutions are not
     guaranteed to be fully surfaced in working unless that route explicitly checks them.
   - Naming normalization (ln→log, csc→cosec) can differ from a mark scheme.

3) Complexity / size caps (hard limits in code)
   - Input length capped (~10k chars), token count capped (~2k), nesting depth capped (~200).
   - Multiple engines use pass-limited rewrite ladders; some expressions need more passes.


INTEGRATION PROGRAM (src/Math/intProgram.py)
--------------------------------------------
Menu modes:
  1 int        indefinite integral via method ladder
  2 de         first-order DE (limited families)
  3 param area area ∫ y dx with parametrics (optional definite bounds)

1) Indefinite integrals — non-elementary / special-function families
   The program prints “no elementary antiderivative” for many non-elementary cases.
   Common families not supported as elementary answers:
     - sqrt(x/(1+x)) and many algebraic irrationals → elliptic/hypergeometric families
     - exp(x^2) → erfi; exp(-x^2) → erf/erfc
     - exp(x)/x → Ei
     - sin(x)/x → Si; cos(x)/x → Ci
     - 1/ln(x) → li
     - ln(1-x)/x, ln(x)/(1-x) → dilog/polylog
     - sin(x^2), cos(x^2) → Fresnel
     - 1/sqrt(1-x^4) → elliptic integral

2) Indefinite integrals — method-family coverage gaps
   Auto routes are: direct/standard, reverse-chain, trig-id, termwise (some),
   division, cyclic parts, substitution, parts, partial fractions, and a narrow
   “poly_quad_exp” family.
   Unsupported / weak forms:
     - u-sub requiring major pre-rewrites (conjugates, completing square, etc.)
     - multi-step substitutions (u then another u) not systematic
     - general Weierstrass t=tan(x/2) rationalization (only partial heuristics)
     - general Euler substitutions for sqrt(ax^2+bx+c)
     - partial fractions requiring irreducible cubic/quartic factors or high multiplicity
     - trig integrals with non-linear arguments (sin(x^2), etc.) unless they hit
       a non-elementary explanation
     - reduction formula families in general (only a few repeated-pattern cases)

3) Differential equations (mode 2) — unsupported families
   - Anything not separable or not linear in the program’s strict first-order form:
     exact DEs, Bernoulli/homogeneous/integrating-factor beyond matched patterns,
     Riccati/Clairaut/Lagrange, higher-order ODEs, systems.
   - Form matching is strict: transformable-but-unrecognized DEs may be rejected.

4) Parametric area (mode 3) — mismatch risks
   - Signed vs absolute area; loop/self-intersection requires splitting (not automatic).
   - Bounds omitted → indefinite in t (many exam questions expect a definite number).
   - Turning-point splitting for positive geometric area not automatic.


ALGEBRA PROGRAM (src/Math/algebraProgram.py)
--------------------------------------------
Menu modes:
  1 cmp, 2 xform, 3 exp, 4 poly, 5 comp sq, 6 solve,
  7 comp, 8 inv, 9 rw, 10 dom/rng, 11 cart, 12 newton

1) Solve (mode 6) — unsupported equation families
   - Trig equations: explicitly out-of-scope (use trigProgram solve).
   - Transcendental equations typically requiring Lambert W / numeric solvers:
       e^x = x, x e^x = k, ln(x)=ax+b, x appears inside+outside log/exp non-linearly.
   - Multi-radical / nested-radical equations:
       sqrt(x+a)+sqrt(x+b)=c, nested radicals, mixtures with log/exp.
   - Higher-root substitutions (cube roots etc.) are not comprehensive.
   - Systems of equations (general simultaneous systems) not supported.
   - Inequalities not supported.
   - Complex roots not returned; “no real solution” possible when only complex exist.

2) Solve (mode 6) — polynomial limits
   - Exact factorised forms are most reliable for low degrees.
   - Higher degrees may be numeric-only or fail to produce closed form.
   - Domain filtering after clearing denominators exists in some cases, but not guaranteed
     for complicated denominators.

3) Newton–Raphson (mode 12) limits
   - Requires differentiable single-variable expression near x0.
   - f'(x)≈0, discontinuities/asymptotes → divergence/failure.
   - Iteration count capped (1–20).
   - May refuse inputs it cannot differentiate reliably.

4) Rewrite (mode 9) limits
   - Target-term driven; if target terms cannot be matched, it errors (“Cannot rewrite…”).

5) Domain/Range (mode 10) limits
   - Hard composite domains/ranges can be incomplete; calculus-based range finding is out of scope.
   - Case-splitting heavy abs/piecewise style not supported.

6) Param→Cartesian (mode 11) limits
   - Best when elimination is linear in the parameter; non-linear eliminations often fail.
   - Multi-branch elimination not systematic.


DIFFERENTIATION PROGRAM (src/Math/deriveProgram.py)
---------------------------------------------------
Menu modes:
  1 n, 2 imp, 3 par, 4 2nd

1) Not supported (structural)
   - Piecewise functions and explicit case analysis (abs chains, sign-dependent results).
   - Partial derivatives / multivariable calculus.
   - Very large/nested expressions may hit token/nesting caps.

2) Timeout-prone / non-exam-presentable families (key weak spot)
   - High-degree integer powers (n ≥ 3) of non-atomic bases (often (ax+b)^n) that occur
     inside trig/exp/log compositions and/or inside large products/quotients.
     These are small in AST size but can explode during simplification.
   - Deep chain+quotient composites such as:
       (sin(poly(x))/((poly(x)^k)+c))^m
     often produce extremely long working lines and can exceed harness timeout.
   - Triple products where each factor is itself a large composite expression can
     produce u', v', w' lines that are not exam-quality for calculator output.

3) Exam guard behaviour (intentional rejection)
   - Some inputs are intentionally rejected with an exam-style message:
       “Too complex for exam-style working … simplify first”
     rather than timing out or printing unusably long lines.


TRIGONOMETRY PROGRAM (src/Math/trigProgram.py)
----------------------------------------------
Solve families supported include direct trig = constant, some factorable products,
some polynomial-in-trig substitution families, and a bounded rewrite ladder.

Unsupported / weak families:
  - Many-angle equations requiring many rounds of sum-to-product / product-to-sum.
  - High-degree trig-polynomials beyond extraction limits (often capped around degree 4).
  - Routes exceeding SOLVE_PASS_LIMIT / FACTOR_REWRITE_PASS_LIMIT (pass-limited ladder).
  - Multi-branch inverse-trig general solutions beyond a few linear patterns.
  - Extra-root filtering is route-specific; some transformations can introduce
    extraneous roots if not explicitly checked.
  - Degree/radian mismatch edge cases if inputs mix contexts.


SUVAT PROGRAM (src/Math/SUVATprogram.py)
----------------------------------------
Purpose: 1D constant-acceleration kinematics with s,u,v,a,t.

Unsupported:
  - Non-constant acceleration problems (a(t), a(v)).
  - Multi-stage motion unless manually split into segments.
  - Vector/projectile mechanics beyond what can be reduced to 1D segments.
Limitations:
  - Requires exactly one target marked with “,” and enough known values.
  - Quadratic branches may require physical reasoning to pick sign (not automated).


BOOLEAN PROGRAM (src/ComputerScience/booleanProgram.py)
-------------------------------------------------------
Syntax supports NOT(,), AND(.), OR(+).

Unsupported:
  - XOR/XNOR/implication as first-class operators.
  - Karnaugh-map minimization, don’t-cares, minterm list inputs.
  - Gate-level “exam narrative” beyond short rule steps.

PROGRAM_LIMITATIONS.txt
CASIO symbolic programs — known "can't handle" classes (2026-04-30)

Scope
-----
This file is a practical inventory of expression / equation TYPES that the current
programs do not solve (or do not present with exam-quality working), based on the
actual routing implemented in:

  - src/Math/algebraProgram.py
  - src/Math/intProgram.py
  - src/Math/deriveProgram.py
  - src/Math/trigProgram.py
  - src/Math/SUVATprogram.py
  - src/ComputerScience/booleanProgram.py

The calculator wrappers in src/calc_files/*.py are thin launchers and inherit the
same limitations.


GLOBAL / CROSS-PROGRAM LIMITATIONS
---------------------------------
1) General
   - The system targets exam-style "Method / Working / Answer" blocks for common
     school/university-precalc patterns, not a full CAS.
   - Multi-variable symbolic problems are mostly out of scope unless they reduce
     to a single chosen variable. (e.g. general systems of equations, PDEs, etc.)
   - Piecewise functions and conditional expressions are not supported.
   - Complex-number solution sets are generally not produced; the solvers mostly
     aim for real solutions and may report "no solution" where only complex roots
     exist.
   - Inequalities are not a primary target (except where domain/range logic or
     interval-filtering is explicitly implemented).

2) "Exam-quality working" (formatting)
   - In many places the program prints compact working that is sufficient to
     justify a method choice, but may omit intermediate algebraic lines a strict
     mark scheme would show (e.g. “expand then collect then factor” shown as one step).
   - Output simplification preferences are heuristic: sometimes results are correct
     but not in the mark scheme’s preferred form (needs manual rewriting).
   - Domain restrictions / excluded values are not always carried through every
     transformation (especially when squaring both sides, clearing denominators,
     or making substitutions).
   - Some programs normalize ln → log (or csc → cosec) for calculator friendliness;
     if your mark scheme is strict about naming, that mismatch can appear.

3) Complexity / size limits (hard limits in code)
   - Input length is capped (typ. 10,000 chars). Very long expressions are rejected.
   - Token count is capped (typ. 2,000 tokens). Very “busy” algebra gets rejected.
   - Nesting depth is capped (typ. 200). Deeply nested function/parenthesis trees
     are rejected or require pre-simplification.
   - Many simplification/rewriting routines are pass-limited (small fixed number of
     rewrite/expand passes). Some expressions need more passes than allowed.


INTEGRATION PROGRAM (src/Math/intProgram.py)
--------------------------------------------
Menu modes:
  1 int        (indefinite integral via method ladder)
  2 de         (first-order DE in limited forms)
  3 param area (area ∫ y dx in parametric form; optional definite bounds)

1) Indefinite integrals: TYPES NOT HANDLED (or deliberately reported as non-elementary)
   A. Non-elementary antiderivatives (common exam “state non-elementary” cases)
      - sqrt(x/(1+x)) and many other algebraic irrationals that reduce to elliptic
        integrals / special functions rather than elementary functions.
      - e^(x^2)  → requires erfi(x) (program prints a non-elementary explanation).
      - sin(x)/x → Si(x) (program prints special-function/series explanation).
      - cos(x)/x → Ci(x) (program prints special-function/series explanation).
      - 1/ln(x)  → li(x) (program prints special-function explanation).
      - sin(x^2), cos(x^2) → Fresnel integrals / series forms (explained).
      - 1/sqrt(1-x^4) → elliptic integral / series (explained).
      Notes:
        - The program’s “no elementary antideriv” output is intentional and aims
          to be exam-appropriate when no limits are supplied.
      Other common non-elementary families (generally out of scope as elementary answers):
        - exp(-x^2) and exp(-a x^2) → erf / erfc families
        - exp(x)/x, exp(a x)/x → Ei(x)
        - sin(x)/x, (1-cos(x))/x → Si/Ci style integrals (some are covered)
        - ln(x)/(1-x), ln(1-x)/x → dilog / polylog families
        - x^x, a^(x^2), exp(exp(x)) → not elementary
        - sqrt(1 ± x^3), 1/sqrt(polynomial degree ≥ 3) → often elliptic/hypergeometric
        Note: a few special-function names are mentioned in the fallback text, but
        the program does not compute/generalize these families systematically.

   B. Integrals requiring advanced substitution beyond the built-in heuristics
      - Non-obvious u-sub where u is not a “near-subexpression” (e.g. nested algebraic
        structures needing multiple preparatory rewrites).
      - Repeated / cyclic substitutions (e.g. some trig-sub chains, Euler substitutions).
      - Integrals that require Weierstrass t = tan(x/2) systematically (only some
        routes approximate this via trig rewrite heuristics).

   C. Method preconditions (common “almost works but not quite” cases)
      - Many trig-integral routes require the trig argument to be linear (ax+b).
        If the argument is non-linear, the trig rewrite ladder will usually stop.
      - Many rational-function / partial-fraction routes assume denominators factor
        over the reals into linear/quadratic factors with manageable multiplicity.
      - Some “recognise derivative of denominator / numerator” patterns require an
        exact linear match; slight algebraic disguises may not be detected.
      - U-substitution heuristics depend on detecting a “good u” candidate:
          - If the right u is not present as a clear subexpression, auto-sub often fails.
          - If u-sub needs a preliminary algebraic rewrite (e.g. splitting fractions,
            multiplying by a conjugate, completing a square), it may not be found.
      - Many rules assume integer powers; fractional/irrational exponents can block
        pattern detection even when the integral is elementary.
      - Absolute value handling is selective: some log-abs patterns are printed,
        but full case analysis for abs inside composite functions is not done.

   D. Integrals requiring advanced methods not implemented
      - Reduction formula problems (e.g. ∫ sin^n x dx, ∫ x^n e^x dx beyond the
        parts ladder’s limited patterns).
      - Integration by series as a primary method (except a few built-in
        non-elementary explanations).
      - Contour / residue / complex methods (out of scope).
      - Improper integrals / convergence arguments (out of scope).
      - Recurrence/reduction formulas in general (only a handful of repeated-pattern
        cases are handled).
      - Euler substitutions for sqrt(ax^2+bx+c) systematically (if a solvable form
        needs this route, it may fail unless it matches another built-in pattern).
      - Trig substitution for general sqrt(a^2-x^2), sqrt(x^2-a^2), sqrt(x^2+a^2)
        in full generality (some instances work; not a complete coverage claim).
      - General rational function of sin/cos using t = tan(x/2) as a universal method.

   E. “Exam-quality working” gaps for integration
      - For some solved integrals, the program may not show the full substitution
        chain (e.g. it may jump from “Let u=…” to the simplified integral quickly).
      - Partial fraction decomposition working may be compacted and not always
        in the exact coefficient style a mark scheme prefers.
      - Constant-of-integration handling is generally “+ C”, but may not show
        absorbing constants between lines.

   F. Route-specific coverage gaps (based on the actual method ladder)
      The auto solver tries a fixed set of route families. If your integral needs
      a method outside these families, it will fail or fall back to “non-elementary”.

      - Direct / standard:
          - Misses integrals that require non-trivial rearrangement before applying
            a standard rule (e.g. clever add/subtract, splitting into telescoping
            parts, or rewriting into a recognized derivative form).
          - Function coverage is limited to a fixed name list; exotic functions
            (special functions) are not integrated.

      - Reverse-chain (detect “outer(u) * u'”):
          - Requires u' to be present as a factor up to a constant multiple.
          - If u' is “hidden” (needs expansion/factorization to expose), it may fail.
          - Multi-step reverse-chain (u-sub then another u-sub) is not systematic.

      - Trig-id route:
          - Focuses on a small set of trig-power/rewrite patterns.
          - High powers of mixed trig functions (sin^m x cos^n x with large m,n)
            may require repeated identities beyond pass limits.
          - Non-linear trig arguments generally block trig power patterns.

      - Substitution (u-sub):
          - “Good u” detection is heuristic; many textbook substitutions are not found
            unless u is an obvious subexpression and simplifies the integrand cleanly.
          - Substitution requiring trig/hyperbolic substitution is not systematic.

      - Parts and cyclic parts:
          - Parts selection is heuristic; some classic parts problems need a very
            specific u/dv choice (or repeated parts) to terminate.
          - Cyclic parts is only triggered for certain recognizable cycles; general
            cycles (involving multiple trig/exp products or nested cycles) may fail.

      - Partial fractions:
          - Requires detecting a rational structure and factoring denominator into
            manageable linear/quadratic factors with limited multiplicity.
          - Denominators with irreducible cubic/quartic factors, or high-degree
            repeated factors, are not reliably decomposed in exam-friendly form.

      - Polynomial division:
          - Only applies when division is detected as “worth it” and pass-limited;
            some rational forms need more aggressive long division + decomposition.

      - Poly-quad-exp special route:
          - Targets a narrow family like polynomial-in-x times exp(quadratic/linear)
            or similarly structured repeated patterns; outside that family, it will
            not help.

2) Differential equations (mode 2): TYPES NOT HANDLED
   - Anything not separable or not linear in the specific first-order sense used.
     In particular:
       - Exact equations M(x,y)dx + N(x,y)dy = 0 (not targeted)
       - Bernoulli / homogeneous / integrating-factor beyond the program’s linear form
       - Logistic-type with algebraic rearrangement not recognized
       - Clairaut / Lagrange / Riccati (not targeted)
       - Higher-order ODEs (second order, etc.)
       - Systems of DEs
   - Initial conditions are only meaningful if the solver successfully matches
     the supported form; otherwise the program prints a generic “no closed-form”.
   - “Exam-quality working” gaps:
       - When a DE is rejected, the explanation is generic; it does not diagnose
         exactness / homogeneity / Bernoulli etc.

   - DE form-matching is strict: if a DE is algebraically transformable into a
     supported form but not syntactically recognised, it may still be rejected.

3) Parametric area (mode 3): TYPES NOT HANDLED / LIKELY TO MIS-MATCH MARK SCHEMES
   - Area requires a consistent interpretation: A = ∫ y dx = ∫ y(t) * x'(t) dt.
     If a mark scheme defines “area” differently (signed vs absolute, loop handling,
     splitting at turning points), outputs can differ even when calculus is correct.
   - Multiple loops / self-intersections: requires splitting into sub-intervals;
     not automatically detected.
   - If bounds are omitted, the result is an indefinite integral in t; many exam
     questions expect a definite numeric area and/or absolute area.
   - “Exam-quality working” gaps:
       - The program prints the key substitution line, but may not split at critical
         points to ensure a positive geometric area.
   - Rewriting/expansion is pass-limited (EXPAND_PASS_LIMIT / TRIG_REWRITE_LIMIT),
     so some integrands won’t be simplified enough to integrate even if a human would.


ALGEBRA PROGRAM (src/Math/algebraProgram.py)
--------------------------------------------
Menu modes:
  1 cmp      (equivalence check with transformation trail)
  2 xform    (transform one expression into another)
  3 exp      (expand)
  4 poly     (polynomial factor / solve style tasks)
  5 comp sq  (complete the square)
  6 solve    (equation solving: polynomial/rational/radical/substitution, real roots)
  7 comp     (compose functions)
  8 inv      (inverse functions, with optional domain constraint)
  9 rw       (rewrite / simplify toward targets)
  10 dom/rng (domain and range)
  11 cart    (parametric → cartesian)
  12 newton  (Newton–Raphson iterations)

1) Solve mode (mode 6): EQUATION TYPES NOT HANDLED
   - Trig equations are explicitly out-of-scope here (the program tells you to use
     trigonometry solve mode).
   - General transcendental equations (mixing exp/log/trig/powers) are not solved
     in closed form. Examples:
       - e^x = x (Lambert W needed)
       - x + ln(x) = 0
       - x = tan(x)
   - Systems of equations (simultaneous equations) are not supported.
   - Inequalities (solve f(x) > 0, etc.) are not supported.
   - Equations whose “main variable” cannot be uniquely chosen (multiple symbols
     with comparable structure) may fail routing.
   - Complex roots are not returned; “no real solution” may be printed where complex
     solutions exist (e.g. x^2 + 1 = 0).
   - High-degree polynomials may be solved numerically internally in some routes,
     but exam-quality factorised forms are not guaranteed beyond low degrees.
   - Domain-exclusion filtering after clearing denominators exists in some cases,
     but may be incomplete for more complex “denominator depends on x” structures.
   - Numeric interval filtering only works if both bounds are numeric; symbolic
     bounds (like pi/2) may not be accepted for filtering in some cases.
   - Substitution solving is limited: some “let u = x^2” style routes exist, but
     general algebraic substitutions (u = g(x) with back-substitution + domain
     filtering) are not comprehensive.
   - Absolute-value equations are handled for some linear forms, but nested abs,
     abs mixed with radicals, or abs in denominators often fall outside.
   - Radical equations: only some structured patterns are supported.
       - Works best when the equation reduces to a quadratic in sqrt(x) (or a
         similarly simple “single radical” substitution).
       - Not handled well:
           - Multiple different radicals at once (e.g. sqrt(x+1)+sqrt(x-1)=3)
           - Higher roots requiring cubic/quartic substitutions (e.g. x^(1/3) forms)
           - Radicals nested inside radicals
           - Radical + log/exp combinations (transcendental with radicals)
   - Transcendental families (typ. require Lambert W / numeric methods) are out of scope:
       - a^(x) = b x + c
       - x e^x = k
       - ln(x) = a x + b
       - x appears both inside and outside a log/exp in a non-linear way

   - Polynomial solving limits:
       - Exact “factorised answer” is most reliable for low degrees (linear/quadratic,
         sometimes cubic/quartic if patterns match).
       - Higher-degree polynomials may only yield numeric roots (or may fail to
         produce any closed form), and the working may not match mark schemes
         expecting factorisation or the rational root theorem steps.

   - Rational equations with parameter-dependent denominators:
       - Clearing denominators can introduce extraneous roots; filtering is done in
         some cases but is not guaranteed for complicated denominators.

2) Newton–Raphson (mode 12): TYPES NOT HANDLED WELL
   - Requires a single-variable expression with a differentiable region near x0.
   - Multiple roots / flat derivative near root (f'(x) ~ 0) can cause divergence,
     huge steps, or non-exam-friendly intermediate values.
   - Functions with discontinuities / vertical asymptotes near iterates will fail
     or produce misleading iterations.
   - Does not automatically justify convergence, bracket the root, or handle
     “show two iterations then 3 d.p.” mark-scheme constraints beyond printing steps.
   - The program may refuse to run Newton–Raphson if it cannot differentiate the
     input expression reliably (it prints “Cannot differentiate this expression…”).
   - Iteration count is capped (1 to 20). Problems requiring many iterations for a
     stable 3 d.p. answer may not fit the mark scheme using this tool alone.

3) Inverse functions (mode 8): TYPES NOT HANDLED
   - Not every function is invertible in elementary terms; outputs can be correct
     but may require stating restrictions manually if the input domain constraint
     is omitted.
   - Multi-branch inverses (e.g. trig inverses beyond principal branch, or functions
     requiring splitting into cases) are not fully supported.
   - Implicitly defined functions or piecewise definitions are not supported.
   - “Exam-quality working” gaps:
       - The solver aims to show y=..., swap x/y, and solve; but may omit explicit
         monotonicity justification unless prompted by a domain constraint.

4) Domain/Range (mode 10): TYPES NOT HANDLED
   - Exact domain/range for complicated composite functions (nested radicals,
     logs, rational + trig mixes) may be incomplete.
   - Range-finding that requires calculus (global min/max over R) is not targeted.
   - Piecewise / absolute-value heavy expressions can be under-analysed.
   - “Exam-quality working” gaps:
       - The program can print domain/range lines, but may not show inequality
         solving steps (e.g. “require radicand ≥ 0”) in full mark-scheme detail.
   - Domain/range reasoning is mostly algebraic; cases requiring calculus (turning
     points / global extrema) or multi-branch case splits can be missed.

5) Expand / factor / rewrite (modes 3/4/9): TYPES NOT HANDLED
   - Full symbolic factorisation over integers/rationals for large multivariate
     polynomials is out of scope.
   - Non-polynomial factorisation (e.g. factoring expressions with exp/log/trig
     into “nice exam factors”) is heuristic and incomplete.
   - Results may be algebraically correct but not in the exact “preferred” target
     form (e.g. sign conventions, factor ordering).
   - Rewrite (“rw”) is target-term driven: if the requested target terms are not
     present / cannot be matched, it errors (“Cannot rewrite in that term.”).

6) Cartesian / parametric conversion (mode 11): TYPES NOT HANDLED
   - Primarily supports cases where eliminating the parameter reduces to a simple
     linear equation in the chosen parameter (then substitute and simplify).
   - Non-linear eliminations (e.g. needing a trig identity in t, multiple branches,
     or higher-degree elimination) often fail or require manual algebra first.


DIFFERENTIATION PROGRAM (src/Math/deriveProgram.py)
---------------------------------------------------
Menu modes:
  1 n    (normal differentiation, includes tangent line request support)
  2 imp  (implicit differentiation for one dependent variable)
  3 par  (parametric differentiation dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt))
  4 2nd  (prints first + second derivative)

1) Expression TYPES NOT HANDLED
   - Piecewise functions, conditional definitions.
   - Derivatives requiring distribution theory (e.g. derivative of |x| at 0 with
     one-sided discussion) are not represented; abs is treated symbolically and
     may yield non-exam-appropriate results without case splits.
   - Multi-variable partial derivatives (∂/∂x, ∂/∂y) are not the target.
   - Vector-valued functions / curvature / arc length etc. not supported.
   - Functions outside the supported name set (sin/cos/tan/sec/cosec/cot, inverses,
     exp/log/log10/sqrt/abs, hyperbolics) will fail parsing/routing.
   - Very large expressions may trigger “too nested” even if mathematically
     differentiable; they need manual simplification first.
   - Derivatives requiring explicit case-splitting for simplification are not shown
     as cases (e.g. sqrt(x^2) simplifications, abs chains, sign-dependent results).

2) Complexity blowups / timeout-prone forms (observed in chaos runs)
   - Very large products with many high powers and deep nesting can trigger
     exponential simplification/expansion costs (or effectively “hang”).
     Typical offenders:
       - Product of many polynomial-power factors times nested trig/exp/log chains.
       - Repeated composition like log(log(abs(ax+b))+c) multiplied by large algebra.
   - In batch runs, these can exceed the per-case timeout (default ~12s in the
     harness), leading to a killed subprocess and blank/partial output.

   - A particularly common blow-up driver is:
       - High-degree integer powers (n ≥ 3) of non-atomic bases (typically (ax+b)^n,
         or nested polynomial bases) *inside* trig/exp/log compositions and/or
         inside large products/quotients.
     Even when the parsed AST is small, simplification (`tidy`) expands these
     powers and can balloon runtime.

3) “Exam-quality working” limitations (derivatives)
   - For very complex products, the program may emit a single gigantic line for
     u', v', w' (or the final dy/dx), which is not exam-quality and not suitable
     for calculator screen width. These should be treated as “not supported for
     exam presentation” unless manually simplified/split.
   - Complex chain+quotient composites (e.g. sin(poly(x)) / (poly(x)^k + const))^m
     can require many intermediate simplifications. Even if computable, the
     working is usually too long to be exam-quality.

2) Implicit differentiation (mode 2): EQUATION TYPES NOT HANDLED
   - Implicit relations with multiple dependent variables (e.g. z(x,y)) or more
     than one derivative symbol in the final expression are not supported.
   - Equations where the chosen dependent variable does not actually depend on the
     independent variable (degenerate cases) are rejected.
   - “Exam-quality working” gaps:
       - The program prints a clean “differentiate both sides” and “rearrange for dy/dx”,
         but does not systematically show product/chain rule expansions term-by-term
         for very large expressions (it may compact steps).

3) Parametric differentiation (mode 3): TYPES NOT HANDLED
   - Requires x(t), y(t) with differentiable expressions; corner points / piecewise
     parameterisations are not supported.
   - If dx/dt = 0 at a point, dy/dx is infinite/undefined; the program may not
     present the “vertical tangent” narrative unless the expression simplifies cleanly.

4) Second derivative (mode 4): WORKING LIMITATIONS
   - Output is correct when the algebra is stable, but “exam-quality” simplification
     (especially trig identities) may not match a mark scheme.
   - When the first derivative is already long, the “differentiate again” working
     is compacted and may omit intermediate simplification steps.


TRIGONOMETRY PROGRAM (src/Math/trigProgram.py)
----------------------------------------------
Public flows (internally): prove, transform, solve, rewrite.

Solve flow: trig equations with interval filtering + general-solution detection.

1) Trig equation TYPES NOT HANDLED / COMMON FAIL CLASSES
   - Equations not reducible to:
       - a single trig function of a linear angle equals a constant (direct routes),
       - a factorable product of such terms,
       - a polynomial in a single trig function u = sin(ax+b), cos(ax+b), tan(ax+b),
         etc. with limited degree (extraction is bounded; polynomial-in-trig degree
         is extracted up to a small limit and solving exact forms is low-degree),
       - a small set of special-case rewrites (double-angle / reciprocal / conjugate).
   - Mixed-angle multi-function equations that require heavy manipulation, e.g.:
       - sin(x) + sin(2x) = 1
       - sin(x) = cos(2x) + 1/2 (sometimes solvable, but not guaranteed)
       - equations requiring sum-to-product / product-to-sum in multiple passes
   - Equations whose solution sets require case-splitting beyond basic factoring
     (e.g. involving abs values of trig functions).
   - Equations requiring symbolic handling of identities across all reals (proof of
     identity) beyond the supported equivalence checks may degrade to numeric checks.

   - Degree / polynomial extraction limits:
       - “Polynomial in trig” extraction is capped (commonly max degree 4 for u).
         Higher-degree trig-polynomials may not be recognized as such.
       - Exact solving is limited to low-degree polynomials; higher degrees may
         fall back to numeric root finding (then mapped back to angles).
   - Rewrite/search is pass-limited (SOLVE_PASS_LIMIT, FACTOR_REWRITE_PASS_LIMIT).
     Some equations need more identity steps than the ladder allows.
   - Inverse-trig equations (asin/acos/atan) are only solved in some linear cases;
     multi-branch general solutions (beyond principal values) are not guaranteed.
   - Equations involving sec/cosec/cot can be harder: solutions may require extra
     exclusions (where sin/cos=0) that are not always surfaced in working.
   - Equations that are solvable but require “domain filtering” for introduced steps
     (e.g. multiplying both sides by sin(x) or cos(x), squaring both sides) may
     produce extra roots unless the route explicitly checks them.

   - Function/operator coverage limits:
       - The solver is built around the standard trig set (sin, cos, tan, sec,
         cosec, cot) plus some inverse/hyperbolic parsing. Equations involving
         less common trig forms (e.g. versin, haversin) are not supported.
       - If equations require converting between degrees and radians mid-working
         or mixing degree/radian contexts, results can be wrong or rejected.

   - “Many-angle” equations:
       - Problems requiring repeated use of sum-to-product / product-to-sum, or
         repeated angle-reduction transforms across multiple different angles, can
         exceed the solve rewrite pass limits.

2) Interval / general solution presentation limitations
   - If the question expects a specific general solution format (e.g. “x = α + 2πn”
     with exact rational multiples of π), some solutions may print as decimals
     depending on the route.
   - For complicated intervals (open/closed endpoints, unusual bounds), solution
     filtering is numeric and can be sensitive to tolerance at endpoints.
   - “Exam-quality working” gaps:
       - The ladder prints the identity it used when it knows it, but for deeper
         rewrites it may skip intermediate algebraic lines that a mark scheme shows.
       - Endpoint handling can be tolerance-sensitive; exact endpoint solutions
         can be missed or duplicated if the interval is tight.


SUVAT / MECHANICS PROGRAM (src/Math/SUVATprogram.py)
----------------------------------------------------
Purpose: constant-acceleration 1D kinematics with variables:
  s (displacement), u (initial velocity), v (final velocity),
  a (acceleration), t (time)

1) Problem TYPES NOT HANDLED
   - Non-constant acceleration (a not constant), e.g. a(t) or a(v) problems.
   - 2D/3D vector mechanics (projectiles, components, bearings) unless manually
     reduced to separate 1D SUVAT problems.
   - Forces / Newton’s 2nd law / energy / momentum questions (not targeted).
   - Multiple-stage motion (piecewise t intervals) unless solved manually as
     separate runs.

2) Input/solution limitations
   - Requires exactly one target marked with "," and sufficient other values.
   - If more than one variable is unknown, it will not print a full consistent set.
   - Quadratic branches (e.g. v = ±sqrt(...)) are produced, but physical branch
     selection (direction reasoning) is not automated.
   - “Exam-quality working” gaps:
       - Units are appended, but sign conventions / direction descriptions are not
         explained (a mark scheme may demand “take upwards positive”, etc.).


BOOLEAN ALGEBRA PROGRAM (src/ComputerScience/booleanProgram.py)
---------------------------------------------------------------
Syntax:
  , for NOT (A,  means NOT A)
  . for AND
  + for OR
  Variables: tokenized into uppercase letters; multi-letter tokens are split into
  implied AND (e.g. AB == A.B) per expand_vars().

1) Expression TYPES NOT HANDLED
   - XOR, XNOR, implication, equivalence operators as first-class tokens (must be
     expressed via AND/OR/NOT expansions manually).
   - “Don’t care” terms and Karnaugh-map style minimization (not implemented).
   - Numeric truth tables / minterm/maxterm list inputs (not the interface).
   - Multi-output logic minimization.
   - If you use lower-case or unexpected characters, parsing fails.

2) “Exam-quality working” limitations
   - Simplification steps are rule-based and can be correct but not match a
     teacher’s preferred law order (e.g. absorption vs De Morgan first).
   - NAND/NOR form conversions are provided, but may not show a full gate-level
     derivation narrative beyond a short step list.


HOW TO USE THIS FILE
--------------------
If you find a question that "fails", categorize it into one of the classes above.
If it is *in scope* but the output is not exam-quality, treat it as a formatting/
working-quality gap (not a math gap) and improve the working lines for that route.
